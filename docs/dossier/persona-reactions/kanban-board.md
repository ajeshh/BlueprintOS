# Reaction: the internal kanban board

## Gut, first sip

Honestly? My stomach did a little drop. Not the good kind.

Because I *know* this move. I've made this move maybe forty times. The project where I get stuck,
the dopamine's gone, and instead of doing the boring next thing I go "you know what would help?
A board." And then I spend an afternoon on the board. Trello, then Notion, then a self-hosted
Plane instance, then a `tasks.md` with a custom renderer, then back to Linear because the
keyboard shortcuts are clean. I have *built kanban boards*. Plural. As procrastination from the
actual work.

So when BOSS — the thing that's supposed to be my conscience — pitches me a board, my first
read is: oh no, you've been infiltrated. The build-trap got to you too.

## "Fire up an HTML site to see it visually"

This is the tell. "Fire up an HTML site" is *exactly* the sentence my brain uses to justify a
two-hour detour. The visual surface is the candy. It's not that visual boards are bad — it's
that for *my* specific failure mode, a new visual surface is another thing to tinker with, style,
re-theme, and abandon. You're handing the build-addict a fresh canvas and calling it project
management.

Would I open it on day 2? Sure. Day 2 is when everything's still shiny. Day 2 I open everything.

Day 9 is the question. Day 9, when the demo works and the thrill's gone and the next thing I
should do is *email the three people who tried it* — does the board help? No. The board will
show me a column of "stuff I could build" and I'll go build the most fun-looking card. The board
optimizes for "what's in flight," and what's in flight is always more building. It literally
renders my disease in three pretty columns.

## What it does NOT solve

My problem is not that I forget what's in place. I *never* forget the cool half-built stuff —
I can recite all 50 repos. My problem is I won't sit with the unglamorous part of *one* of them
long enough for it to become real. A board doesn't make me do the unglamorous part. It makes the
glamorous part more visible, which is the opposite of what I need.

"Help people remember what's in place visually" — that's a real need for *some* cohort. A team.
Someone juggling six client projects. Not me. I'm solo and the issue is never memory, it's
follow-through.

## The thing that would've made me lean in (and didn't happen here)

If the board surfaced *staleness as pressure* instead of *options as candy*, that's different.
Like — a column that's literally "things you started and walked away from," with the days-since
counter ticking up, and BOSS being a bit of a jerk about it. "This is your fourth new card this
week. The thing from Tuesday has zero users and you haven't talked to one. Want to look at it?"
That's a board that knows what I am. That's the override-pattern energy — the tool refusing to
let me launder avoidance as productivity.

But a Trello-but-inside-BOSS? That's a feature that *any* tool has. It doesn't do one thing
sharply, it adds a surface. And BOSS's whole pitch to me was that it's not trying to be
everything — it's the conscience, the seasoned hand who calls my BS. A generic board is the
seasoned hand handing me a coloring book.

## Does it violate BOSS's own principles?

Feels like it brushes against #2 (just-in-time, never premature ceremony). A kanban board *is*
ceremony. For a Quickstart-mode solo idea, a board is premature — it's the org chart for a
company of one. The principle says support shows up when the project earns it. A board on day 1
of a throwaway is exactly the premature-ceremony anti-pattern.

## Net

- **Excites me?** For about 90 seconds, yes — and that 90 seconds is the warning sign, not the
  signal. The excitement is "ooh new surface to build," which is the trap talking.
- **Would I use it?** I'd set it up. I would not still be looking at it in two weeks. I have a
  graveyard of boards to prove it.
- **Helps my problem or feeds it?** Feeds it. Cleanly. It's a build-trap with a Trello skin.

Don't build me a board. Build me the thing that *notices* I'm reaching for the board instead of
the customer. That I'd actually respect.

> **Design signal:** the bounce here is the product. A prolific shipper's "ooh, a board!" reflex
> is the addiction lighting up. A conscience tool should treat that reflex as a symptom to
> reflect back, not a need to satisfy. The interesting version of this feature is one that makes
> *staleness* and *avoidance* visible — not *options*.
